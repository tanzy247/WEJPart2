Tin Can Bay

Here we got to hand feed dolphins. This was a really cool and informative experience. The pod of dolphins come from ancestors from the 1950â€™s. It all started when one sick dolphin visited the bay and was fed by a fisherman. Days later he returned with his family. For a long time the locals fed the pod; however, overtime this had evolved and is now a regulated project. The volunteers running the project know all of the dolphins that visit and can feed them 2-3kg of fish only - so they are not reliant and still need to hunt for their food. Sadly, 2 of the dolphins had harsh shark bites on them from recent attacks, but luckily they survived.

Bundaberg

Whilst in Bundaberg we visited Mon Repos Turtle Centre, which is a globally significant site that supports the largest concentration of nesting marine turtles on the eastern Australian mainland, and the most significant loggerhead turtle nesting population in the South Pacific region. The success of the loggerhead nesting and hatching turtles at Mon Repos is critical for the survival of this endangered species.

Currently it is the end of the nesting season and the beginning of the hatching season; however, due to the overlap we were lucky enough to see both.

We did however have to wait until 11pm which was way past my bed time but totally worth it.

These hatchlings were part of a nest dig where the rangers dig a nest that has hatched 2 days prior to check to see if there are anymore turtles and account for the success of the nest.

Bundaberg

When the mum comes ashore, she finds a dark place to build her nest and digs a hole around 60cm deep. This is where she then starts to lay her eggs. Once she has finished laying she fills the hole and covers up her tracks before entering the ocean again. This whole process takes around an hour.

Bundaberg

The majority of the time the nests are left exactly as they are; however, sometimes they are relocated if the mum has nested in a poor location. Unfortunately, this was the case for us which meant the ranger had to take out each of the eggs and later they were re-buried in a safer place where they would have a higher success of surviving.

Koorana Crocodile Farm

A great afternoon visiting the crocodile farm and learning about the crocodiles and how/why they are farmed. The project was actually government initiated in order to stop illegal hunting, which was causing the species to become endangered. They are now farmed and are only used at the end of life.

They are more fun to watch than to taste!
